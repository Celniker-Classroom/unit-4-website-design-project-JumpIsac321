Electromagnetism

Electrostatics

Electrostatics is a simplified version of electrodynamics where charges are assumed to remain still and the electric field is calculated around it. The electric field around a charged particle is calculated using coulomb's law, and when multiple charges are present, the total electric field is calculated by finding the sum of the electric field from each individual charge. A different way of doing electrostatics is with poisson's equation, which calculates the electric potential instead of electric field at each point in space.

Maxwell's equations

When going from electrostatics to electrodynamics, you need to start using maxwell's equations, a set of 4 equations which are: Gauss's law for the electric field, Gauss's law for the magnetic field, Faraday's law, and Ampere's law.

  • Gauss's law for the electric field: The total field that goes in and out of a volume is proportional to the total amount of charge inside.
  • Gauss's law for the magnetic field: The total magnetic field that goes in and out of a volume is always zero, or equivialently, there are no individual north poles or south poles.
  • Faraday's law: A changing magnetic field causes the electric field to go in a loop.
  • Ampere's law: A current or changing electric field causes the magnetic field to go in a loop.

Electromagnetic waves

In a vacuum where there are no charges or currents, combining the equations together leads to electromagnetic waves. Electromagnetic waves involve both the electric and magnetic fields forming a wave and moving through space. The electric field and magnetic field both oscilate perpendicular to the direction of travel, and both fields are also perpendicular to each other. They also travel at the speed of light, and light is a type of electromagnetic wave.
