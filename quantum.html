Quantum Mechanics

Particles as Waves

The fundamental idea in quantum mechanics is that particles behave like waves. This is shown by the double slit experiment where waves that pass through create an interference pattern which occurs when waves interact when themselves, but when particles go through the experiment, they also make an interference pattern, making them a wave. The particle's wave is called the wavefunction.

Probabilities in Quantum Mechanics

Quanutm mechanics says that given the initial state of a system, it isn't possible to predict what happens to it with absolute certainty. It only gives probabilities of each possibility happening. These probabilities can be calculated from the wave nature of the particle, where the peaks of the wave coresponds to areas of high probability.

Schrodinger equation

Given an initial state for the wavefunction, the schrodinger equation gives how the wave changes over time, until the particle is measured, where the wavefunction will collapse, meaning that the wavefunction all goes onto a single point, so that the particle is certainly at that position. Where the wavefunction collapses to depends on where the peaks are.

The uncertainty principle

Some values have an uncertainty relation between them, meaning that there is a limit to how much you can know about certain values. For example, there is an uncertainty relation between position and momentum, so if you know the position exactly, the momentum is completely uncertain and can be anything, and if you know the momentum exactly, then the position is completely uncertain and can be anything. Other values do not have an uncertainty relation, like momentum and energy, meaning that you can know both values exactly at the same time. The uncertainty relation does not mean that given a completely known position it is impossible to measure the momentum, it means that the momentum is fundamentally undefined and has no single value.
